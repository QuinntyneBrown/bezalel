# Theory of change

The hypothesis for how FaithTech Toronto's Bezalel activities could produce lasting outcomes, stated as inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes.

## Inputs

- Technologists seeking to invest their skill in mission-aligned work
- Churches, ministries, and mission-driven nonprofits with real, unmet operational or ministry problems
- FaithTech Toronto's community, events infrastructure, and convening power
- Practitioner time, mentorship, and architectural expertise
- Backing-partner funding and in-kind support for projects that mission partners receive free of charge

## Activities

- Running Create programs that form practitioners and surface partner problems ([`docs/playbooks/create-night-format.md`](../playbooks/create-night-format.md))
- Running hackathons that prototype against surfaced problems ([`docs/playbooks/hackathon-to-engagement.md`](../playbooks/hackathon-to-engagement.md))
- Running discovery to validate and scope the most promising prototypes ([`docs/playbooks/running-a-discovery.md`](../playbooks/running-a-discovery.md), [`docs/playbooks/scoping-an-engagement.md`](../playbooks/scoping-an-engagement.md))
- Selecting projects based on redemptive fit, mission-partner commitment, team capacity, and available backing
- Running partner-backed continuation builds that take a validated prototype toward durable, maintained software without charging the mission partner ([`docs/model/offerings.md`](../model/offerings.md))

## Outputs

- Formed practitioners with real project experience and a network of like-minded technologists
- A pipeline of validated, mission-aligned problem statements
- Working prototypes from each hackathon cycle
- Durable, documented, handed-off software for mission partners whose projects continue through the model
- A growing public record of projects, decisions, and evidence about whether the model works (this repository)

## Outcomes

- Partners run technology that measurably improves their ability to serve their mission, without taking on unsustainable technical debt
- Technologists find and stay in a durable community where skill and calling are integrated, rather than treated as separate
- A credible, repeatable, partner-backed model for free redemptive technology work exists that other FaithTech chapters or similar communities can adapt
- Trust compounds: partners refer other partners, practitioners refer other practitioners, and the flywheel accelerates
