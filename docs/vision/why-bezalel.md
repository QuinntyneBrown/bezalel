# Why Bezalel

The name, the calling, and the specific gap Bezalel fills.

## The name

Bezalel is named after the craftsman in Exodus 31:1–5, the first person Scripture describes as filled with the Spirit of God — not for prophecy or leadership, but for skilled making: "wisdom, understanding, knowledge, and all kinds of skill," to design and build with gold, silver, bronze, stone, and wood.

The thesis this points to: redemptive work can be expressed through skilled craft, not only through explicitly "spiritual" activity. Building well — with real engineering discipline — is itself a legitimate, sufficient expression of the mission. Bezalel doesn't need to disguise itself as a ministry program to matter; it needs to be excellent at making things.

## The gap Bezalel fills

Three things already exist in this space, and none of them alone solves the problem:

- **A normal dev shop** can build well-architected software, but has no pipeline of mission-aligned problems, no formation process for practitioners who want their skill to matter, and typically prices partners out or treats them as a standard client.
- **A one-off hackathon** surfaces energy, real problems, and fast prototypes, but by design does not produce durable software. Most hackathon projects die within weeks because there is no path from "it demoed well" to "it is deployed, documented, and maintained."
- **A volunteer-only ministry tech effort** has goodwill and proximity to the problem, but usually lacks the engineering rigor, availability, and continuity to ship and sustain real systems — leading to the fragile, unmaintained software described in [`MANIFESTO.md`](../../MANIFESTO.md).

Bezalel exists to close that gap: it is the missing middle step between a hackathon prototype and a durable product, staffed by practitioners who were formed through FaithTech Create, working against problems that were already validated as real and mission-aligned before an engagement ever starts. See [`docs/model/the-pipeline.md`](../model/the-pipeline.md) for how that hand-off actually works.
