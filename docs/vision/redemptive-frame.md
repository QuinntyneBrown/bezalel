# The redemptive frame

An operational definition of "redemptive," so the term does the same work every time it's used in this repository.

## Working definition

A piece of technology work is **redemptive** if it meets all three:

1. **It targets real repair.** It addresses something specifically broken, neglected, exploited, or excluded — not a generic feature request. There is a before-state that is genuinely worse than the after-state for a specific, identifiable group of people.
2. **It is built with integrity of means.** The way it is built does not itself exploit anyone — not the end users, not the practitioners donating their skill, not the partner organization's limited resources. See [`docs/model/guardrails.md`](../model/guardrails.md).
3. **It is built to last.** A prototype that repairs something for a weekend and then breaks is not redemptive in the sense Bezalel means — durability is part of the definition, not an optional extra. This is why craftsmanship is treated as inseparable from the mission in [`MANIFESTO.md`](../../MANIFESTO.md).

## What this rules out

- Technology that is merely "for a good cause" but extracts data, labor, or money in ways the people involved wouldn't consent to if they understood it.
- Technology that is well-built but aimed at nothing in particular — competent work with no specific repair target isn't redemptive, it's just good engineering (which Bezalel also values, but names separately).
- Technology that is well-aimed but abandoned at prototype stage, leaving the partner worse off (holding a half-built system they now depend on and can't maintain).

## How this gets applied

Every engagement should be able to answer, in one paragraph: who is worse off today, what specifically will be different for them, and why the team building it can be trusted to finish and sustain it. The [engagement template](../engagements/_template.md) has an explicit "redemptive angle" field for this reason.
