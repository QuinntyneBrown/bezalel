# 0001. Adopt the Bezalel name

## Status

Accepted

## Context

FaithTech Toronto needed a working name for an exploration: could the community take selected hackathon prototypes toward durable, well-architected software for church, ministry, and nonprofit partners, free of charge and with the backing needed to sustain serious craft? The experiment needed an identity distinct enough to describe the continuation model while remaining part of FaithTech Toronto rather than becoming a consultancy.

## Decision

Use **Bezalel** as the working name for this FaithTech Toronto experiment, after the craftsman in Exodus 31:1–5 — the first person Scripture describes as filled with the Spirit of God, specifically for skilled making: "wisdom, understanding, knowledge, and all kinds of skill." The name is chosen to make an explicit claim: that redemptive work can be fully expressed through craftsmanship itself, and that excellence in building is not in tension with the mission but is one of its primary vehicles.

See [`docs/vision/why-bezalel.md`](../vision/why-bezalel.md) for the full rationale and the specific gap this fills relative to a normal dev shop or a one-off hackathon.

## Consequences

- Bezalel names a model being explored by FaithTech Toronto, not a consultancy, vendor, or separate organization.
- The ThoughtWorks comparison communicates a standard of product and engineering practice, not a fee-for-service business model.
- Mission partners receive the work free of charge, so continuation depends on practitioner capacity and backing partners rather than client demand.
- Bezalel is positioned as a craftsmanship-first initiative, which raises the bar for what work is allowed to carry the name — see [`docs/model/guardrails.md`](../model/guardrails.md).
- The name requires ongoing explanation for audiences unfamiliar with the Exodus reference; onboarding and README materials carry this context so it isn't lost.
- Naming the initiative after a biblical craftsman commits Bezalel to being explicit about its faith-rooted origin, even while aiming to be credible to skeptical secular technologists.
