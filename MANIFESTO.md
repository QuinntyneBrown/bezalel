# The Bezalel manifesto

This is the point of view behind the Bezalel experiment — read it to decide if you want to help test it.

## Why redemptive tech

Most technology is built to extract: attention, money, data, compliance. Some technology is built to help, but stops at good intentions and ships something fragile, unmaintained, or unfinished. FaithTech Toronto is exploring Bezalel because churches, ministries, and mission-driven nonprofits deserve better than either — they deserve technology built with the same rigor a serious company would demand of its own product, in service of ends that are actually good.

We call this **redemptive technology**: technology that repairs, restores, or dignifies something that is broken, neglected, or excluded — and that is built well enough to actually do that, sustainably, over time.

## Not a consultancy

Bezalel is not an agency, vendor, or consultancy for hire. It is a FaithTech Toronto team exploring a community model: Create forms practitioners and surfaces real problems, hackathons test possible responses, and selected projects receive disciplined follow-through. Mission partners are not clients and are not charged for the work. Backing partners and practitioners make the work possible through funding, time, tools, mentorship, and other in-kind support.

The shorthand "ThoughtWorks for redemptive technology" describes an aspiration for craft: multidisciplinary teams, technical excellence, honest discovery, disciplined delivery, and responsible handoff. It does not describe the commercial model. Free work is still selective and bounded by mission fit, team capacity, and available backing; it is not an on-demand development service.

## Three postures toward technology

It helps to name the difference between three postures, because they are often confused for one another:

- **Exploitative** — technology optimized for extraction: engagement farming, dark patterns, data harvesting, labor exploitation. The user or the world is the raw material.
- **Merely-ethical** — technology that avoids doing harm. It doesn't exploit anyone, but it also doesn't aim at anything beyond neutrality. Most "ethical tech" conversations stop here.
- **Redemptive** — technology that actively moves something toward repair: it restores dignity, rebuilds trust, reconnects what was isolated, or makes a good thing sustainable that would otherwise fail. It is not neutral — it is aimed.

The Bezalel model only makes sense in the third category. The aim is not to build tools that merely avoid harm, but to build tools that repair something.

## Craftsmanship is non-negotiable

Good intentions are not a substitute for good architecture. A prayer app that crashes, a donor database that leaks data, a volunteer scheduler nobody can maintain after the hackathon ends — these do not serve the mission, no matter how well-meant. They *cost* the mission: trust, time, money, credibility with the very technologists we need to attract.

Bezalel's founding conviction is that the mission is served *through* the craft, not despite it. This is where the name comes from — see [`docs/vision/why-bezalel.md`](docs/vision/why-bezalel.md) for the full rationale. The short version: Scripture's first named example of someone filled with the Spirit for a task describes a craftsman, not a preacher. Skilled making is treated as a spiritual vocation in its own right.

Practically, this means:

- We do not ship prototypes as if they were finished products, and we do not call finished products "good enough" if they are not maintainable.
- We borrow the engineering discipline associated with teams like ThoughtWorks — architecture review, real testing, real documentation, and real handoff plans — because mission partners cannot afford technical debt the way a funded startup can.
- We treat "it demoed well at the hackathon" as a starting signal, not a finish line. See [`docs/model/the-pipeline.md`](docs/model/the-pipeline.md) for what has to be true before a prototype becomes a partner-backed build.

## Who this is for

This document is meant to be read by a competent, possibly skeptical technologist who has other places they could spend their time. If you're weighing whether this is one more well-meaning-but-underbuilt "tech for good" effort: that is exactly the failure mode this experiment is organizing against. Read [`docs/model/guardrails.md`](docs/model/guardrails.md) for the lines the model holds, and [`docs/engagements/`](docs/engagements/) for what the team has actually shipped once there is something there.
