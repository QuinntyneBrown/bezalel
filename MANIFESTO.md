# The Bezalel manifesto

This is the point of view behind Bezalel — read it to decide if you're in.

## Why redemptive tech

Most technology is built to extract: attention, money, data, compliance. Some technology is built to help, but stops at good intentions and ships something fragile, unmaintained, or unfinished. Bezalel exists because churches, ministries, and mission-driven nonprofits deserve better than either — they deserve technology built with the same rigor a serious company would demand of its own product, in service of ends that are actually good.

We call this **redemptive technology**: technology that repairs, restores, or dignifies something that is broken, neglected, or excluded — and that is built well enough to actually do that, sustainably, over time.

## Three postures toward technology

It helps to name the difference between three postures, because they are often confused for one another:

- **Exploitative** — technology optimized for extraction: engagement farming, dark patterns, data harvesting, labor exploitation. The user or the world is the raw material.
- **Merely-ethical** — technology that avoids doing harm. It doesn't exploit anyone, but it also doesn't aim at anything beyond neutrality. Most "ethical tech" conversations stop here.
- **Redemptive** — technology that actively moves something toward repair: it restores dignity, rebuilds trust, reconnects what was isolated, or makes a good thing sustainable that would otherwise fail. It is not neutral — it is aimed.

Bezalel only works in the third category. We are not interested in building tools that merely avoid harm; we are interested in building tools that repair something.

## Craftsmanship is non-negotiable

Good intentions are not a substitute for good architecture. A prayer app that crashes, a donor database that leaks data, a volunteer scheduler nobody can maintain after the hackathon ends — these do not serve the mission, no matter how well-meant. They *cost* the mission: trust, time, money, credibility with the very technologists we need to attract.

Bezalel's founding conviction is that the mission is served *through* the craft, not despite it. This is where the name comes from — see [`docs/vision/why-bezalel.md`](docs/vision/why-bezalel.md) for the full rationale. The short version: Scripture's first named example of someone filled with the Spirit for a task describes a craftsman, not a preacher. Skilled making is treated as a spiritual vocation in its own right.

Practically, this means:

- We do not ship prototypes as if they were finished products, and we do not call finished products "good enough" if they are not maintainable.
- We hold ourselves to the engineering standards of the best secular consultancies — architecture reviews, real testing, real documentation, real handoff plans — because the partners we serve cannot afford technical debt the way a funded startup can.
- We treat "it demoed well at the hackathon" as a starting signal, not a finish line. See [`docs/model/the-pipeline.md`](docs/model/the-pipeline.md) for what has to be true before a prototype becomes an engagement.

## Who this is for

This document is meant to be read by a competent, possibly skeptical technologist who has other places they could spend their time. If you're weighing whether this is one more well-meaning-but-underbuilt "tech for good" effort: that is exactly the failure mode we are organizing against. Read [`docs/model/guardrails.md`](docs/model/guardrails.md) for the lines we hold, and [`docs/engagements/`](docs/engagements/) for what we've actually shipped once there is something there.
